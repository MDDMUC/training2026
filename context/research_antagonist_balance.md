# Antagonist Balance for Climbers — Finger Extensors, Shoulder ER, Scapular Stability

Researched 2026-05-28. Saved for future-session reference.

## The concept in one paragraph

Climbing is a pulling sport. Every move pulls the fingers into flexion, the humerus into internal rotation and adduction, and the scapulae into downward rotation and depression. Over years, this asymmetric loading creates measurable strength imbalances between agonist (pulling) and antagonist (pushing / opening) muscle groups — and those imbalances correlate with the sport's signature overuse injuries: medial epicondylitis ("climber's elbow"), subacromial impingement, scapular dyskinesis, and the chronic forward-shoulder posture. The climber-specific antagonist groups worth training are: **finger extensors** vs flexors, **shoulder external rotators** (infraspinatus, teres minor, posterior deltoid) vs internal rotators, **serratus anterior + scapular protractors** vs retractors, and **lower trapezius** vs the dominant upper trap. Antagonist work is not optional cosmetic balance — it's a load-tolerance and longevity strategy, particularly past 40.

## Key findings

### Push:pull ratio — qualitative, not numeric

The widely repeated "Hörst 1:2 push:pull ratio" is climbing-media folklore. Hörst's actual prescription is more modest: on antagonist days, **match sets per session** between pushing and pulling movements at the same body angle (vertical push = vertical pull, horizontal push = horizontal pull). Hooper, Vagy, and Steven Low all argue for **qualitative horizontal balance** — pair every pulling vector with an opposing pushing vector across the week — rather than chasing a fixed numeric ratio. Hooper's stated target is **~1:1 in volume terms**, especially for climbers over 40.

### Finger flexion:extension ratio is measurable and trainable

Devise et al. (2023, *Frontiers in Sports and Active Living*) measured the finger flexion-to-extension MVC ratio in climbers vs untrained controls:

- **Climbers' mean ratio: 6.27** (95% CI 5.94–6.61)
- **Untrained controls: ~3.7**
- Climbers' flexors are roughly **70% stronger** than untrained controls'; their extensors are essentially **untrained**.
- Their 4-week RCT compared **extensor-isolated** training vs **paired flexor+extensor** training, both at 80% MES (maximum extensor strength), 2× per week, ~15-minute sessions.
- **Only the extensor-isolated group rebalanced the ratio.** Paired training did not shift the ratio — the flexors grew alongside the extensors and the gap stayed open.
- The implication mirrors Tyler Nelson's asymmetry-rule for fingers: to close a gap, you train the weaker side **in isolation**, at relative intensity, with matched volume.

### Shoulder external-rotation deficit is the signature climber asymmetry

Wong & Ng (2009, *Journal of Athletic Training*) measured isokinetic shoulder rotator strength in climbers vs non-climbers:

- **Climbers' ER:IR ratio: 0.79**
- **Non-climbers' ER:IR ratio: 1.03**
- p < .001
- Climbers have weaker external rotators *relative to* their internal rotators — the same pattern seen in throwing athletes and linked to impingement and labral pathology.
- The corrective stimulus is **strength-load external rotation training** (not band warm-up doses). 2 kg lateral raises and face pulls are activation, not training stimulus.

### Tyler Twist for medial and lateral epicondylitis

Tyler et al. (2014, *Int J Sports Phys Ther*) demonstrated that **eccentric wrist flexion/extension via the Theraband FlexBar** resolved previously failed cases of golfer's elbow:

- **Daily, 3 × 15 eccentric reps, 6+ weeks**
- Pain and grip strength both improved significantly
- The mechanism is loaded eccentric remodeling of the common flexor/extensor tendon at the epicondyle.
- The AAOS / OrthoInfo Rotator Cuff Conditioning Program is the parallel daily protocol for shoulder prehab.

### Serratus anterior and the push-up plus

Decker et al. and Cools et al. (2003, *JOSPT*) used surface EMG to rank serratus-anterior exercises:

- The **push-up plus** (push-up followed by maximal scapular protraction at the top) produced the **highest serratus EMG** of any exercise tested.
- Serratus drives scapular upward rotation and protraction; weakness here is the proximate cause of scapular winging and downstream impingement.
- Vagy and Esther Smith both prescribe push-up plus as the first-line serratus exercise.

### Lower trap is selectively trained by the prone Y

Hwang & Jeon (2024, *Isokinetics and Exercise Science*) compared upper vs lower trapezius EMG during Y-raise variants:

- **Prone Y (face-down, thumbs up, arms in Y position) produced the highest lower-trap:upper-trap activation ratio.**
- The dominant climbing upper trap masks lower trap weakness; reverse fly and face pull are **upper-trap-dominant** and do not correct the imbalance.
- Lower trap drives scapular depression and upward rotation — both essential for overhead shoulder health.

### Sleeper stretch is contested

Laudner et al. (2013, *JOSPT*) compared modified sleeper stretch vs cross-body stretch for internal-rotation ROM:

- **Both improved ROM** to a similar degree.
- The **cross-body stretch is safer** for non-throwers (less anterior shoulder compression).
- For climbers, **cross-body is the default**; sleeper stretch is optional and should be modified to reduce anterior load.

### Climbing-injury epidemiology

Schöffl & Schöffl (2020, *MLTJ*) — the standard reference for climbing injury patterns:

- Finger pulleys are the #1 acute injury.
- **Medial epicondylitis, lateral epicondylitis, and subacromial impingement** dominate the chronic / overuse category.
- All three are antagonist-imbalance injuries — they cluster in climbers who do not train finger extensors, external rotators, or scapular stabilizers.

## Protocol details by muscle group

### Finger extensors

- **Devise protocol:** 80% MES, 2× per week, isolated extensor work, ~15 min sessions, 4 weeks to measurable rebalance.
- **Practical substitute when no graded device is available:** rubber bands of progressive thickness, 3 × 15 reps, 2× per week. Progress band thickness when the current band hits 15 reps × 3 sets at low RPE.
- A **graded extensor device** (Tindeq extensor attachment, FlexBar, or a calibrated rubber-band kit) is the future upgrade — it enables % MES prescription matching Devise's methodology.

### Shoulder external rotators

- **Band ER at 0° abduction**, elbow at side, 3 × 10 per side, 2× per week, at a load where the last 2 reps are challenging.
- Progress to **band ER at 90° abduction** (the climbing-specific position) after 4–6 weeks.
- The standard "no money" warm-up dose is **activation**, not training stimulus. Strength gains require load.

### Serratus / scapular protraction

- **Push-up plus:** 3 × 10–12, 2× per week. Add load (weighted vest or band around back) when bodyweight becomes easy.
- **Bear-crawl protraction holds** as a supplementary option.

### Lower trapezius

- **Prone Y raise:** 2 × 12, 2× per week. Light dumbbell (1–2 kg) or no load — selectivity matters more than load here.
- The **prone T** (horizontal abduction) is upper-trap and rhomboid dominant; do not substitute.

### Elbow tendon prophylaxis

- **Tyler Twist (FlexBar) — daily, 3 × 15 eccentric**, 6+ weeks for resolution of symptoms; ongoing as maintenance.
- Without a FlexBar: extensor wrist curls 3 × 12, slow eccentric tempo, 3× per week.

## Gap analysis — current Tuesday push session

The athlete's existing Tuesday session:

- **Vertical push superset:** Dips 3×7 + OHP 30 kg 3×7
- **Horizontal "push" superset:** Row 50 kg 3×7 + Bench 55 kg 3×7
- **Antagonist + prehab:** Lateral raises 4×14 @ 2 kg, Face pulls 2×15, Reverse fly 2×12
- **Forearm balance:** extensor + flexor wrist curls
- **Single-leg knee (ACL):** Bulgarian splits + step-ups
- **Lattice deep-squat mobility**

Identified gaps:

1. **No finger extensor training at strength load.** Wrist extensor curls train the wrist extensors, not the finger extensors (extensor digitorum). Devise's protocol specifically isolates finger extension. This is the most consequential gap.
2. **No external rotation at strength load.** Face pulls are upper-back work with incidental ER; 2 kg lateral raises are warm-up doses. Neither closes the 0.79 ER:IR deficit. Band ER at 0° with a challenging load is missing.
3. **No dedicated serratus / scapular protraction.** Bench press involves serratus only at end-range protraction, which most lifters cut short. Push-up plus is absent.
4. **Lower trap is under-dosed.** Reverse fly is upper-trap and rhomboid dominant. Prone Y is the lower-trap-selective exercise and it's not in the session.
5. **No bilateral posterior chain.** Bulgarian splits and step-ups train the quad and glute medius; a hip-hinge pattern (RDL or single-leg RDL) is missing — relevant for hamstring tone and ACL maturity.
6. **Lateral raises 4×14 are over-dosed.** 56 reps at 2 kg is volume without stimulus — mid-delt is already worked by OHP and dips. Trim and reallocate.
7. **Generic flexor wrist curl is redundant.** Climbing volume already overloads finger and wrist flexors; explicit flexor curls add fatigue without rebalancing anything.

## Recommendations

### Important caveat — kit limitation

The athlete currently has **no graded finger-extensor device** (no Tindeq extensor attachment, no Theraband FlexBar). The Devise protocol's 80% MES prescription requires a force-reading device. Until that's addressed, the protocol scales around **rubber bands of progressive thickness** — train to a reps-in-reserve target (last 2 reps challenging) and progress band thickness when the current band becomes easy. A graded device is the highest-value future kit upgrade for this athlete; it unlocks both Devise-style finger extensor prescription and Tyler Twist–style elbow prophylaxis with measurable progression.

### Add to Tuesday session

- **Rubber-band finger extensions** — 3 × 15, progress band thickness over weeks. Also apply on Thursday for the 2×/week Devise frequency.
- **Band ER at 0° abduction** — 3 × 10 per side, at a load where the last 2 reps are challenging. This is the strength-dose ER work the session is missing.
- **Push-up plus** — 3 × 10–12. Highest-EMG serratus exercise per Cools et al.
- **Prone Y raise** — 2 × 12, light load. Lower-trap-selective per Hwang & Jeon.
- **Single-leg RDL** — 3 × 8 per side. Bilateral-equivalent posterior chain stimulus that also supports ACL maturity through controlled single-leg hip hinge.

### Modify existing items

- **Lateral raises:** 4×14 @ 2 kg → **2×12**. Trim the over-dose; reallocate the time to ER and finger extensor work.
- **Reverse fly:** 2×12 → **1×12**. Offset by adding prone Y, which covers lower trap better.
- **Drop generic flexor wrist curl.** Climbing already overloads flexors; the session time is better spent on extensor isolation.

### Future kit upgrade — flagged

- **Theraband FlexBar (green or red)** would enable the Tyler Twist daily protocol — 3×15 eccentric daily, 6+ weeks — which is the gold standard for medial and lateral epicondylitis prophylaxis in climbers. For now, the extensor wrist curls plus rubber-band finger extensions cover the prehab mechanism at lower fidelity.
- **Tindeq extensor attachment** (or equivalent calibrated extensor jig) would enable Devise's % MES prescription with measurable rebalance tracking every 4 weeks.

### Frequency and timing

- **Finger extensor work: Tuesday + Thursday** (matches Devise's 2× per week).
- **ER, push-up plus, prone Y: Tuesday only** is sufficient at strength load; the warm-up dose continues on every climbing day via Hooper's 3-step warm-up.

### Net session impact

Estimated added time: **~8 minutes**. Removed time from lateral raise / reverse fly trim and dropped flexor curl: **~5 minutes**. **Net Tuesday session length increase: ~3 minutes** for substantially better antagonist coverage.
