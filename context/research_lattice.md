# Lattice Training — Methodology Notes

Researched 2026-05-28 from latticetraining.com and partner sources. Lattice is a UK climbing-training company (Tom Randall, Ollie Torr, Maddy Cope) with the largest published climber-assessment dataset.

## Assessment battery — what they test

**Lattice's three core physical metrics** (their published 2023+ predictor data for sport climbers, n=436):

| Test | What it measures | Edge / setup |
|---|---|---|
| 7-sec max hang | Maximal finger strength | 20 mm flat edge, half crimp, both arms, max added weight held cleanly for 7 s. Score = (BW + added) / BW × 100% |
| 2-rep weighted pull-up | Upper-body max strength | Max load you can pull 2 clean reps with. Score = (BW + added) / BW × 100% |
| Critical Force (4-min test) | Forearm aerobic endurance | 24 × (7 s pull / 3 s rest) on 20 mm edge, one arm. Force you can sustain ≈ aerobic ceiling. |

**Sport-climbing benchmarks (men, % bodyweight)**

| Grade | Max hang (2-arm) | 2-rep pull-up | Critical Force |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7c–8b+ | 138–164% | 140–155% | 42–56% |
| 8c–9a  | 170–190% | 158–167% | 56–73% |

(Lattice publishes wider ranges in different posts; these are the predictor-paper bands.)

**Finger strength is the #1 single predictor.** R = 0.66 with best-route-worked, explains ~43.6% of variance. Pull-up strength and CF add the rest.

## Max-hang protocol (Lattice's recommended hangboard work)

- **Intensity:** 80–95% of max total load. Beginners stay 80–85%; advanced reach 90–95%.
- **Hang duration:** 5–10 s per rep.
- **Reps total:** 6–8 hangs per session.
- **Rest:** 2–5 min between reps.
- **Typical session:** 6 × 10 s @ 85% with 2 min rest. Always warm up first; never max-hang on fatigued fingers.

## Weighted pull-up max strength

Same percentages logic — work in the 80–90% 1RM range, low reps (3–5), 3–5 sets, 3–5 min rest. Lattice frames pull-up as a secondary but important contributor to sport-climbing performance.

## Periodization stance

Lattice does not publish a single fixed cycle template; their app (Crimpd) builds individualised plans. The published guidance for a structured block:

- **Base / preparation** — volume up, lower intensity, technical climbing
- **Strength** — max hangs, weighted pulls, low-volume bouldering
- **Power / power-endurance** — convert strength into climbing-specific output
- **Performance / peak** — drop training volume, climb on goals
- **Deload** weeks every 3rd–4th week or autoregulated by RPE

They emphasise **autoregulation by RPE** rather than rigid prescription, and use bodyweight + training-RPE logs to fine-tune.

## On older climbers (40+)

Lattice's "Older climbers" article makes these points explicit:
- Recovery takes longer — drop session frequency before dropping intensity if forced to choose.
- Bias toward **technique and skill** sessions over pure strength volume.
- Compound strength lifts (squat, deadlift, pull) **remain valuable** — don't drop them.
- Sleep 7–9 h, mobility daily, manage trips and deload around them.
- Individualisation > generic protocols.

## Asymmetry research (Lattice blog)

Most climbers have small bilateral asymmetries (~1.6 kg average). A larger gap warrants targeted unilateral work on the weaker hand — load it first in sessions, match volume not load on the dominant side until the gap closes.

## Key takeaways for Martin

1. **Run the 7-sec max hang test on 20 mm and the 2-rep pull-up test** as the two primary benchmarks every 4 weeks. They are the metrics Lattice's research supports best.
2. Martin's current numbers (one-arm equivalent — R 55 kg / L 52 kg on 20 mm; pull-up ≈ 30 kg added 1RM at 82 kg BW = ~136% BW for a single rep, lower for 2-rep) land him at the **entry of the 7c–8b** band on Lattice's curve. Goal of 55 kg added pull-up = ~167% BW for 1 rep, putting him near the 8b+ benchmark.
3. **Max-hang dose to drive finger strength:** 6 × 7–10 s @ 85% MVC, 2–5 min rest, 2× per week is the proven Lattice template.
4. **Critical Force is worth adding once per cycle** as a sport-climbing-specific endurance marker, especially given his 8a sport focus.
5. **At 43, prefer 2×/week heavy sessions over 3×/week** if volume forces a choice. Recovery is the bottleneck.
6. **For the left-hand asymmetry**, train the weak side first while fresh; match volume not load on the stronger side until the gap narrows.
