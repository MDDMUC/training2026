# No-Hangs (Abrahangs) — Emil Abrahamsson + Keith Baar tendon research

Researched 2026-05-28. Saved for future-session reference.

## The concept in one paragraph

"No-hangs" (popularized by Swedish climber Emil Abrahamsson as **Abrahangs**) are a low-intensity, high-frequency finger-loading protocol where you stand under a hangboard with feet on the ground and **pull only as hard as needed to feel a light strain** in your forearms — roughly **40% of your maximum**. Two short sessions per day (~10 minutes each), separated by at least 6 hours, performed daily over weeks. The premise: tendon cells (tenocytes) adapt to short, repeated, sub-maximal stimuli — not to maximal strain.

## The science behind it

**Keith Baar (UC Davis)** — the underlying tendon researcher. Key findings:

1. **Refractory period.** When you load a tendon, tenocytes synthesize collagen for ~6 hours, then become unresponsive to further loading. Doing a second heavy session within that window doesn't add stimulus.
2. **Saturation point.** Even within a single session, stimulus saturates at about **10 minutes of load**. More load ≠ more adaptation — once you've triggered the signal, doing more is wasted effort (and adds joint stress).
3. **Two sessions per day with ≥6 hours rest** is optimal — you get one stimulus, then another fresh stimulus.
4. **Collagen + vitamin C 30–60 min before loading** provides building blocks for synthesis. Without vitamin C, synthesis is impaired.
5. **Isometrics specifically** drive the tendon adaptation more than eccentrics for the muscle-tendon junction.

**The original Abrahamsson protocol (YouTube video, 30-day version):**
- 2 sessions per day, separated by ≥6 hours
- ~10 minutes per session
- 10 reps × (10 s on / 50 s rest)
- Intensity: feet on floor, **~40% MVC** (light strain only)
- Multiple grip positions across sessions: 4-finger open, front-3 open, front-2 open, half-crimp variants
- Edge: 14 mm in the original; safer at 20 mm or 25 mm

**The peer-reviewed study (Gilmore, Abrahamsson, Baar 2024):**
- Retrospective analysis of 500+ climbers from the Crimpd app
- Compared: heavy max-hangs only · Abrahangs only · both combined · neither
- **Abrahangs-only group: ~2.5–3.2% strength gain over 4–16 weeks** (Cohen's d ≈ 0.29, small effect)
- **Combined group (heavy + Abrahangs): best result** — additive
- Authors *speculate* the mechanism is connective-tissue adaptation but did **not** measure tendon thickness, stiffness, or biopsies
- Self-selected intensity, retrospective design — limits causal claims

**Tyler Nelson's critique (Camp 4 HP):**
- Skeptical of the mechanism story — no imaging or biopsies
- Effect size is small
- No verification of actual load (participants self-rated 40%)
- Calls it a useful adjunct, not a magic protocol

## How it relates to what we already have

Martin's Phase 1 currently includes:
- **Mon — Density Hangs at 75% MVC** (Nelson's protocol, 6 sets, ~12 min). Tendon-and-MTU-density stimulus.
- **Thu — Asymmetry isos at 70% MVC** (Tindeq, 4 sets per hand, ~8 min). Targeted weaker-side work.
- **Climbing on Wed + Sat** — incidental finger loading.

Abrahangs sit **below** these in intensity (~40% vs 70–75%). They're not duplicative — they target a different adaptation (collagen synthesis at low load) than the Density and Max Hangs (force production + MTU density). The 2024 study found **combining the two is best** — which makes sense biologically.

## The catch for Martin specifically

1. **Healed left A2 pulley.** Low-load, high-frequency work is the safest possible stimulus for a healing pulley. This is the *strongest* argument for adding Abrahangs.
2. **Refractory-period collisions.** Doing Abrahangs the same day as Density Hangs (Mon) or Asymmetry Isos (Thu) wastes the second session — the tendon is already in its 6-hour refractory window. Schedule around heavy days.
3. **Time burden.** 2 × 10 min daily = 20 min/day = ~140 min/week. Adds up.
4. **24-hour pain rule still applies.** Even at 40% MVC, 14 weekly sessions is a high training volume. Watch the L middle finger.
5. **Small expected gain.** ~2.5–3.2% strength over 4–16 weeks — meaningful for a serious athlete chasing fine margins, but won't transform the cycle.
